    Imagine This

    You are a UX Designer and you're assigned to start work on a new Design Project. Typically, You start from a blank page in Figma, and you start structuring your design file to ensure that things are in order. For example, fonts, color schemes, design systems, and structuring your page names. This takes time.

    Afterwards, you finally start your design project, and start designing things. Every Designer has a slightly different design process, and it becomes a bigger problem when you need to collaborate with other UX Designers. So, you need to agree on the exact process you are going to take. This takes time.

    From an organizational standpoint, this becomes difficult to view the design projects in Figma as there is no consistency in place across all Designers. There is also a lot of duplicated efforts spent in establishing a new UX Design project each time.

    How might we:

  • Reduce duplicated efforts in starting a new Design Project?
  • Ensure UX Designers are following a structured Design Approach?

    • 1. Communicating the Vision

    To generate anticipation and excitement, I sent emails to all our designers, outlining a clear and compelling vision for the new project I was leading that would improve and standardize our Design Processes in Figma. I explained how the proposed Figma template would revolutionize our workflow, streamline processes, and enhance the quality of our designs. I also requested their future assistance in its development, and the purpose of this was to gain early buy-in and let UX Designers know that this would be a collaborative effort.

    2. Initial Interviews

    To kickstart this change, I began by conducting several open ended 1 on 1 interviews with UX designers to understand their design process and the challenges they faced. I discovered issues like design inconsistencies, project delays, and the frustration of designers dealing with repetitive tasks involved with starting a new UX Design project. From this, I received many actionable insights on what it should contain, and as well as what processes should be embedded in it. We proceeded to base the template structure upon The Double Diamond Design Process model. It is a design thinking method which follows 4 stages, Discover, Define, Develop, and Deliver.

    3. Development

    From the initial interviews, I started developing a prototype version on the general structure and flow for the template from scratch. I often asked and received help from enthusiastic UX Designers on how to create certain assets on the Figma environment. During the early development process, I conducted quick 1 on 1 feedback sessions with 3 UX Designers such that I could quickly iterate and improve the Design Template.

    4. User Testing

    When the Design Template started looking fairly polished, I conducted 2 intensive rounds of user testing with over 12 UX Designers. The purpose was to gather qualitative and quantitative data regarding the template and make modifications based upon it. For example, I would provide them scenario workflows and I would record how long they took a certain action. Afterwards, I would then ask them follow-up questions on that specific workflow for what they would normally do instead. With this information, I was able to further optimize the Design Template.

    5. Beta Testing

    After fine-tuning the Design Template after the User Testing, I initiated a Beta Testing stage and invited around 13 UX Designers who I previously showed the template to try it out for a 4 week period and to provide feedback. After a 4 week period, I had a follow-up 1 on 1 meeting with each of them and they were able to share many insights that I merged into a final copy of the Design Template.

    6. Launch

    Due to our strategic actions early in the process of building excitement for the Design Template, many UX Designers expressed eagerness to utilize it. Unintentionally, several UX Designers from the beta test group, who had initial access to the Design Template, generously shared it with their colleagues on the team. As a result, by the official launch date, a substantial number of UX Designers had already gained access to it. I believe the adoption process proceeded smoothly due to the open communication early on, discussing the adoption of the Design Template, and the collaborative efforts involved in its development with Designers from various teams.

    7. Reflections & Takeaways

    This was a good starting project for me because I spent a good portion of time learning about Figma and how to use it. I gained empathy for UX Designers and what they did by learning how to use the same tools they used, and I am proud to say I know most of Figma in-and-out at this point.
