    The initiative

    In the last 5 years, the overall SAP HD&A (HANA Data & Analytics) software development organization has witnessed rapid expansion, resulting in a substantial increase in the number of UX design projects and teams. While the overall development organization has adopted the SAFe project management framework, the HD&A Design organization's project management practices have remained static. Historically, the Design organization has never had dedicated project managers, leading to project management responsibilities being handled by Design Managers, which often falls beyond their typical expertise.

    This misalignment between our organization's growth and the absence of updated project management processes has given rise to several critical challenges, including inefficiencies in project execution, suboptimal resource allocation, breakdowns in communication, and difficulties in prioritizing Design backlog items.

    Daniel's Role:

  • Daniel supported the project management within a 2 week sprint environment and facilitated team discussions for stand-up meetings (3x/week), sprint reviews, and retrospectives.
  • Responsible for forecasting Sprint iterations with the development team and stakeholders. He managed the queue of deliverables, adjusting priorities according to the needs of the business on the sprint board within the project management software, Jira.
  • Worked with the Product Owner to investigate the list of Jira tickets within the backlog
  • Crafted the Launch and engagement strategy project to invite the existing customer base onto the customer forum/support site. This consisted of working closely with Marketing and managing deliverables, personally creating and managing the A/B test invitation email campaigns, and executed upon an engagement strategy to keep users engaged for the first 2 months of the launch.

    • 1. Problem Identification and Stakeholder Engagement:

    Problem Framing (Lean Canvas): We began by framing the problem statement using a Lean Canvas approach. This involved identifying key pain points in the current project management process within the UX Design organization. To do this, we engaged with various stakeholders, including Design Managers, UX Designers, Developers, and Product Managers.

    Stakeholder Interviews (Empathy Mapping): To truly understand the challenges faced by different stakeholders, we conducted empathy mapping sessions. These sessions allowed us to empathize with their perspectives, pain points, and expectations. Through open and candid discussions, we unearthed valuable insights.

    Journey Mapping: We mapped out the entire project lifecycle, from project initiation to delivery. This process involved visualizing each stakeholder's journey and pinpointing areas where inefficiencies and breakdowns occurred. For example, we identified bottlenecks in the handover of design deliverables to developers.

    2. Solution Design and Iteration:

    Prototyping (Storyboarding): To illustrate the proposed changes, we created prototypes and storyboards depicting the new project management processes. These visual representations helped stakeholders visualize the future state and provided a tangible framework for discussions.

    Feedback Loops (Agile Feedback Framework): Continuous feedback was essential in refining our proposed solutions. We implemented an Agile feedback framework, where stakeholders could provide feedback on the prototypes, and we iterated accordingly. This iterative approach ensured that the final solution was well-received and met the needs of all stakeholders.

    3. Implementation and Change Management:

    Agile Transformation (Scrum Framework): As part of the implementation phase, we transitioned to an Agile project management approach, adopting the Scrum framework. This involved establishing Scrum teams, defining roles and responsibilities, and conducting Scrum training sessions for team members.

    Change Management Plan (ADKAR Model): We recognized that changing established processes would require effective change management. We employed the ADKAR (Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement) model to guide our change management efforts. This model allowed us to systematically address the five critical elements of change, ensuring that team members were not only aware of the changes but also motivated and equipped to adapt.

    Pilot Projects: To minimize disruptions, we initiated pilot projects to test the new project management processes on a smaller scale. This approach allowed us to iron out any unforeseen issues and gather feedback from real-world scenarios before rolling out the changes organization-wide.

    4. Evaluation and Continuous Improvement:

    KPIs and Metrics (Key Performance Indicators): To measure the impact of the initiative, we defined key performance indicators related to project timelines, resource utilization, and communication effectiveness. Regularly tracking these metrics provided insights into the initiative's success.

    Retrospectives (Agile Retrospective Framework): We conducted retrospectives at the end of each project cycle to reflect on what worked well and what could be improved. This Agile practice fostered a culture of continuous improvement, allowing us to fine-tune our processes based on real-world experiences.

    5. Scaling and Integration:

    Scaling the Initiative: After the successful pilot projects and continuous improvement efforts, we gradually scaled the project management optimization initiative across the entire HD&A Design organization. This involved additional training sessions, onboarding of new project managers, and integration with existing SAP processes.

    Integration with SAFe Framework: To ensure alignment with the broader SAP SAFe framework, we worked closely with SAFe experts within the organization to integrate our Agile project management practices seamlessly.

    Reflections

    In conclusion, the journey of optimizing project management for UX design at SAP involved a systematic and collaborative approach. It began with a deep understanding of the problem through Lean Canvas, empathy mapping, and journey mapping. The solution design phase leveraged design thinking, prototyping, and iterative feedback loops. Implementation was guided by Agile principles and change management strategies. Continuous evaluation and improvement, as well as scaling and integration, were essential components of the project's success. This holistic approach ensured that the initiative not only addressed the identified challenges but also fostered a culture of continuous improvement within the organization.
